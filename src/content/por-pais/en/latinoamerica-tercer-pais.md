## First: do you have (or can you get) a European passport?

If you have Italian, Spanish, Portuguese, or other EU ancestry, or can
access European nationality through another route, that path is far
simpler: no quotas, no proof that a local candidate couldn't fill the
role, and you can follow this site's universal guides on L/B/C permits
instead. It's worth investigating before assuming you need the
third-country route, which is much more restrictive.

## How the third-country system works

Unlike an EU/EFTA citizen, a Latin American citizen without a European
passport falls under the **third-country** regime, which in
Switzerland works as follows:

- **Annual quotas (contingents)**: the Federal Council sets a limited
  number of L and B permits for third-country nationals each year,
  split among cantons. They run out — sometimes before the year ends.
- **Priority for the local labour market (extended Inländervorrang)**:
  your future employer must prove to the authorities that they couldn't
  find anyone in Switzerland, the EU, or the EEA to fill the position
  before hiring you. This means the vacancy normally already went
  through a posting-and-screening process for local candidates.
- **Required profile**: in practice, approvals go almost exclusively to
  "highly qualified specialists" (hochqualifizierte
  Fachkräfte/qualified professionals) — typically a university degree
  plus significant relevant experience, in areas where Switzerland has
  a genuine talent shortage: IT/software development, engineering,
  certain medical and scientific specialties, and highly technical
  profiles hard to fill locally.

## The process, step by step

1. **Get a job offer first.** You can't enter as a tourist and look for
   work from inside the country to later regularize your status — the
   third-country permit is processed **before** you travel.
2. **The employer applies for the authorization**, not you directly:
   they request the work permit from the canton, which assesses the
   available quota and the labour market priority test. If the canton
   approves, the file goes up to SEM (the State Secretariat for
   Migration) at federal level for final sign-off.
3. **You apply for the national type D visa** at the Swiss embassy or
   consulate in your country of residence, once you have cantonal and
   federal approval in hand — not before.
4. **You travel already holding the approved visa** and register at
   your destination municipality within 14 days, same as any other
   permit.

The whole process (job offer + cantonal approval + federal approval +
visa) can take several months — plan with margin if you have a specific
contract start date in mind.

## Which sectors have real chances

- **IT and software development**: the sector with the most real
  approvals for third-country nationals, especially in Zurich, Zug, and
  Geneva (many tech and financial-engineering companies compete for
  scarce talent).
- **Engineering and sciences** (especially with a PhD or research
  specialization).
- **Very specific medical profiles** in areas with shortages, though
  credential recognition is a separate, lengthy process.

Entry-level roles, positions without a university requirement, or
sectors with high local supply (general admin, generic marketing, etc.)
almost never survive the labour market priority test for third
countries.

## Language

Although many tech companies in Zurich or Geneva operate internally in
English, the visa process itself and daily life outside the office
(housing, paperwork, social life) go much more smoothly with German,
French, or Italian depending on the region. It's not a legal requirement
for the permit, but it's a real practical advantage — and sometimes
decisive against other candidates.

## Family reunification

Once settled with a B permit as a specialist, you can apply to bring
your partner and children, subject to conditions on adequate housing
and sufficient financial means — this is a separate process, usually
handled after your own settlement.

## Important notice

This guide is informational, not immigration advice. Quotas, the list
of priority sectors, and the exact "highly qualified" criteria are set
each year by the Federal Council and applied by each canton with its
own nuances. Always confirm your specific case with your future
employer (who initiates the process) or with the migration office of
the canton where the job would be based.
