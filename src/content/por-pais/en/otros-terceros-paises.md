## What determines your situation? Your passport, not your continent

What determines your immigration process in Switzerland isn't your
specific nationality or which region of the world you're from, but
whether you hold a passport from an EU/EFTA country or not. If you're
from the United States, the United Kingdom, Canada, Australia, India,
South Africa, or any other country outside the EU/EFTA, you fall under
the same **third-country** regime — same as someone from Latin America,
Asia, or Africa without a European passport.

If you also have (or can obtain) a passport from an EU/EFTA country
through ancestry or another route, that path is far simpler: check this
site's universal guides on L/B/C permits instead of this one.

## How the third-country system works

- **Annual quotas (contingents)**: the Federal Council sets a limited
  number of L and B permits for third-country nationals each year, split
  among cantons. They run out — sometimes before the year ends.
- **Priority for the local labour market (extended Inländervorrang)**:
  your future employer must prove to the authorities that they couldn't
  find anyone in Switzerland, the EU, or the EEA to fill the position
  before hiring you.
- **Required profile**: in practice, approvals go almost exclusively to
  "highly qualified specialists" — typically a university degree plus
  significant relevant experience, in areas where Switzerland has a
  genuine talent shortage: IT/software development, engineering, certain
  medical and scientific specialties.

## The process, step by step

1. **Get a job offer first.** You can't enter as a tourist and look for
   work from inside the country to later regularize your status — the
   third-country permit is processed **before** you travel.
2. **The employer applies for the authorization**, not you directly:
   they request the work permit from the canton, which assesses the
   available quota and the labour market priority test. If the canton
   approves, the file goes up to SEM (the State Secretariat for
   Migration) at federal level.
3. **You apply for the national type D visa** at the Swiss embassy or
   consulate in your country of residence, once you have cantonal and
   federal approval in hand.
4. **You travel already holding the approved visa** and register at your
   destination municipality within 14 days.

## Which sectors have real chances

IT and software development (especially Zurich, Zug, and Geneva),
engineering and sciences (especially with a PhD or research
specialization), and very specific medical profiles in areas with
shortages. Entry-level roles or sectors with high local supply almost
never survive the labour market priority test for third countries.

## Important notice

This guide is informational, not immigration advice. Quotas and the
exact "highly qualified" criteria are set each year by the Federal
Council and applied by each canton with its own nuances. Always confirm
your specific case with your future employer (who initiates the
process) or with the migration office of the canton where the job would
be based.
