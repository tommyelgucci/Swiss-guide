## First: is your profession regulated in Switzerland?

The key distinction is whether your profession is on the list of
**regulated professions** — where the law requires a recognized
qualification to practise legally — or not:

- **Regulated**: medicine, nursing and other healthcare professions,
  teaching (all levels), law, architecture, some technical engineering
  fields, psychotherapy, among others. Without official recognition,
  you can't legally practise in Switzerland, even with years of
  experience.
- **Not regulated**: most IT, marketing, business, and design roles.
  There's no mandatory official process here — your employer evaluates
  your training and experience directly when hiring you.

## If your profession is regulated

The responsible body depends on the sector:

- **Healthcare professions** (doctors, nurses, pharmacists,
  physiotherapists, etc.): the Medical Professions Commission (MEBEKO),
  under the Federal Office of Public Health.
- **Most others** (teaching, technical professions, etc.): the State
  Secretariat for Education, Research and Innovation (SBFI/SERI).
- **Law**: each cantonal bar association, with its own rules depending
  on whether you're from the EU/EFTA (simpler regime) or a third
  country.

The process involves submitting certified copies of your degree, your
study programme, and official translations if they're not in a Swiss
national language. The body compares your training against the
equivalent Swiss qualification — if there are substantial differences,
you may be asked to sit a supplementary exam, complete a practical
adaptation period, or take extra courses before full equivalence is
granted.

## EU/EFTA vs. third-country degrees

For regulated professions, EU/EFTA qualifications usually benefit from
simpler and faster mutual recognition mechanisms (Switzerland-EU
bilateral agreements). Third-country qualifications go through an
individual case-by-case assessment, generally longer and more likely to
require an aptitude test or adaptation period.

## If your profession isn't regulated

There's no official process you need to complete to work, but it can
still be worth it:

- Some employers explicitly ask for an academic equivalence assessment
  during hiring or to set your salary level.
- If you later want to study further in Switzerland (a master's, say),
  the university may ask for an academic equivalence assessment —
  different from professional recognition — via
  swissuniversities/swiss ENIC.

## Important notice

The responsible bodies, timelines, and fees vary by profession and
change over time. Before assuming you do (or don't) need recognition,
confirm your specific case at sbfi.admin.ch or directly with your
profession's regulatory body.
