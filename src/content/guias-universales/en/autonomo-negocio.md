## First: does your permit allow self-employment?

- **EU/EFTA citizens with a B or C permit**: can set up as
  self-employed relatively freely, without needing an additional
  business-specific authorization.
- **Permit L (EU/EFTA)**: more restricted, depends on the permit's
  original purpose.
- **Third-country nationals**: need a specific authorization to work
  self-employed, generally harder to get than a salaried job offer. The
  canton usually requires a viable business plan demonstrating a clear
  economic benefit for Switzerland (job creation, investment, etc.)
  before approving the status change.
- **Permit C** (any nationality, after years of residence): generally
  the most flexible for going independent, with no additional
  permit-related restrictions.

## Choosing a legal form

- **Sole proprietorship (Einzelunternehmen)**: the simplest and most
  common option for starting out alone. No minimum capital required.
  You must register in the Commercial Register (Handelsregister) if
  your annual revenue exceeds CHF 100,000; below that, registration is
  optional.
- **GmbH / Sàrl** (limited liability company): requires a minimum
  capital of CHF 20,000, fully paid in. Separates your personal assets
  from the company's.
- **AG / SA** (corporation): minimum capital of CHF 100,000 (at least
  CHF 50,000 paid in). A more complex structure, common for larger
  businesses or those seeking investors.

## Registering as self-employed with AHV

This is a key step that's often overlooked: for your cantonal
compensation office to officially recognize you as self-employed
(rather than treating it as a disguised employment relationship with a
single "client"), you generally need to show that:

- You work for several clients, not just one.
- You bear the business's economic risk yourself (your own investment,
  possibility of losses).
- You decide how, when, and with what means you work.

Without this recognition, you keep contributing as if you had a single
employer, which has different tax and insurance implications.

## Other things to arrange

- **Insurance**: as self-employed, you must arrange your own accident
  insurance (mandatory) and, if you want, a voluntary second pillar
  (BVG) — there's no employer managing it for you.
- **Taxes**: your self-employment income is declared as personal
  income (ordinary progressive taxation), not as Quellensteuer.
- **Sector licences**: some sectors (food service, healthcare,
  transport, construction) require specific cantonal permits or
  certifications before you can operate.
- **Support**: cantonal economic development offices
  (Standortförderung / promotion économique) usually offer free
  guidance for people starting a business, including step-by-step
  cantonal guides.

## Important notice

The exact permit requirements, minimum capital, and procedures vary by
canton and by your nationality, and change over time. Before taking the
step, confirm your case with the cantonal migration office (for the
permit) and with an advisor or the local chamber of commerce (for the
legal and tax side).
