## Portals by job type

- **General**: jobs.ch, indeed.ch, jobup.ch
- **Public sector / administration**: each canton publishes its own
  vacancies on its own website
- **Banking/finance**: LinkedIn tends to be more effective than generic
  portals
- **Tech/IT profiles**: besides LinkedIn, many companies post directly on
  their own careers page — it's worth checking target companies one by one

## Cleaning, food service, construction, and driving

These are the sectors where newcomers most often land their first job,
especially before they speak the local language fluently or have prior
Swiss work experience: cleaning (Reinigung/nettoyage), unqualified food
service roles (dishwasher, kitchen helper, waiter/waitress or "service"),
construction (labourer, construction helper), and driving (delivery
driver).

**Temp/staffing agencies**: a large share of this hiring goes through
staffing agencies rather than direct postings from the final employer.
The best-known ones, with offices in nearly every canton: Adecco,
Manpower, Randstad, and Coople (a gig/temp-work app widely used for food
service, events, and cleaning). Also search on your own for "temp agency
+ [your canton]" (e.g. "Zeitarbeit Zürich", "travail temporaire Genève",
"lavoro interinale Ticino") — many agencies are local or regional and
don't show up in the generalist portals.

**RAV and Job-Room**: if you register as a jobseeker at the RAV
(Regionales Arbeitsvermittlungszentrum) in your canton, you get access to
Job-Room (job-room.ch), the Confederation's official portal. Ask your RAV
directly whether they can help you find work — they also assist
jobseekers who aren't receiving unemployment benefits.

**Turn on a "JobAgent"**: on jobs.ch and other portals you can set up an
automatic email alert ("JobAgent") with your search criteria — it notifies
you as soon as something new is posted, useful in sectors where openings
fill up fast.

Even when the job doesn't formally require it, picking up the local
language of the canton where you'll live — even at a basic level — makes
a real difference in interviews for these kinds of roles.

## Unsolicited applications (Initiativbewerbung/candidature spontanée)

In Switzerland, it's very normal to write directly to a company you're
interested in even if it hasn't posted an opening. A typical application
dossier includes:

1. Cover letter (1 page, tailored to that specific company)
2. CV (ideally 2 pages, with a photo — unlike the US/UK, a photo on the CV
   is still standard practice in Switzerland)
3. Copies of diplomas and previous work certificates
   (Arbeitszeugnis/certificat de travail)

## The work certificate (Arbeitszeugnis)

Every Swiss employer is legally required to issue this document when an
employment relationship ends, written in a coded style: certain
seemingly-neutral phrases are actually negative signals to those who know
how to read them. If you work in Switzerland, always keep your previous
work certificates — you'll be asked for them in every future application.

## Language level

While many international companies operate in English, the local cantonal
language (German, French, or Italian) is often decisive outside Zurich,
Geneva, or Basel, even for technical hiring processes.
