## The deadline: 12 months on your foreign licence

Once you register as a resident in Switzerland, you can keep driving on
your foreign licence (together with your residence permit) for up to
**12 months**. After that, you need to have exchanged it for a Swiss
licence to keep driving. Don't wait until the last month: if your
country doesn't have a recognition agreement (see below), the exam-based
process can take weeks.

## If your licence is from the EU/EFTA: a straightforward exchange

If your licence was issued by an EU/EFTA country, the exchange is purely
administrative:

- No theory or practical exam.
- You submit your original licence, your residence permit, a photo, and
  a form at the cantonal road traffic office (Strassenverkehrsamt /
  office de la circulation routière / ufficio della circolazione) of
  your canton of residence.
- Your foreign licence is retained (or punched/cancelled) once the Swiss
  one is issued — you can't keep both.
- The process is usually resolved within a few weeks by mail.

## If your licence is from a third country: it depends on the bilateral agreement

Switzerland has bilateral recognition agreements with a limited group of
third countries (among others, Japan, South Korea, Monaco, San Marino,
and a few more). If your country is on that list, the exchange is
administrative, similar to the EU/EFTA process.

If your country does **not** have an agreement — the case for most
third countries, including the United States, Canada, and most of Latin
America, Asia, and Africa — you need:

1. **A theory exam** (questions on Swiss traffic rules, can be taken in
   several languages including English depending on the canton).
2. **A practical driving exam** with a certified instructor, to
   demonstrate you drive to Swiss standards.
3. Meeting the usual medical requirements (sometimes an eye test).

That means budgeting time (prep lessons if needed) and money (exam fees
plus lessons), and planning well ahead of your 12-month deadline.

## How the process works

1. Contact the cantonal road traffic office of your canton of
   residence — each canton manages its own process and exam schedule.
2. Gather the documents: original foreign licence (with an official
   translation if it's not in a Swiss national language or English),
   residence permit, passport photo, and the application form.
3. If an exam applies to you, book an appointment — in high-demand
   cantons (Zurich, Geneva) there can be a waiting list of several
   weeks.

## Important notice

The bilateral agreements and exact requirements change over time and
vary slightly by canton. Always confirm the current list of countries
with an agreement and the exact procedure with the cantonal road traffic
office of your canton of residence, or at astra.admin.ch (Federal
Roads Office).
