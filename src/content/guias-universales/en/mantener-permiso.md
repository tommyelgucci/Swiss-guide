## The 6-month-away rule

If you leave Switzerland for more than **6 consecutive months** without
notifying the migration office (Art. 61 FNIA/AIG), your B or C permit is
automatically extinguished when you return — nobody has to revoke it, it
simply lapses. The fix is simple but has to happen **before you leave**:
ask your cantonal migration office for an absence extension (it keeps your
permit "frozen" during the trip). Without that prior request, coming back
"just before" hitting 6 months doesn't protect you either: the authorities
can also look at where your actual centre of life
(Lebensmittelpunkt/centre de vie) is — if you spend most of the year
abroad even without ever reaching 6 consecutive months, renewal can still
be questioned.

## Myth vs. reality

- **Myth**: "If I come back for a weekend before hitting 6 months, the
  clock resets and nothing happens."
  **Reality**: false. Without a prior authorized extension, the risk of
  the permit lapsing still exists, and returning just in time doesn't
  erase the absence pattern if the authorities review your case at
  renewal.
- **Myth**: "Losing your job automatically means losing your permit."
  **Reality**: it depends on how long you'd been working and your
  nationality. EU/EFTA citizens with more than 12 months of contributions
  keep their right of residence while receiving unemployment benefits;
  with less than 12 months, the permit can be reduced to a jobseeker
  status (usually 6 months). For third-country nationals, cantonal
  conditions are stricter — check with your migration office before your
  contract ends, not after.

## Changing canton

It's not automatic: you must deregister (Abmeldung) from the canton you're
leaving and register (Anmeldung) in the new one within the usual deadlines
(the same 14 days as when you first arrived). The new canton can
re-evaluate the conditions of your B permit (financial means, health
insurance, etc.), so a canton change isn't just an address update — it can
reopen a full review of your situation.

## The path from B to C

The C permit is granted after a number of years of continuous residence
(5 or 10 depending on nationality and bilateral agreement — see the
permits guide). "Continuous" is the key word: long absences or periods
without stable employment can extend the timeline or lead the authorities
to ask for more justification, even if you never technically broke the
6-month rule.

## Important notice

This guide is informational, not legal advice. Federal law (FNIA/AIG)
sets the general framework, but each canton — sometimes each
municipality — applies its own nuances and internal deadlines. Before a
long absence, a job change, or a canton change, confirm your specific
case with the relevant migration office.
